Skill Cards — Progressions & Regressions

Tier 1

2 Min Dead Hang

The absolute foundation of every pulling and hanging movement. Builds grip, shoulder girdle stability, and decompresses the spine. Do this before anything else.

Prerequisites

  • None — start here

Regression Ladder

  • Assisted hang (feet on box, partial weight) — 30 s
  • Full passive hang — 30 s → 60 s → 90 s
  • Scapular hang (depress & retract shoulders) — adds active component
  • Full 2 min active dead hang (shoulders packed)
Key cue: "Pack the shoulders" — don't hang passively on the joints. Actively pull the shoulder blades down and together. This is what makes it useful.
Tier 1

Deep Squat (Heels on Ground)

The human resting position. Without this you cannot progress to Cossack squats or pistol squats, and your backflip will always be underpowered.

Prerequisites

  • None — start here

Regression Ladder

  • Heels elevated on plates — 2–5 cm elevation, work depth
  • Assisted squat (hold TRX / door frame) — go as deep as possible
  • Box squat to deep position — sit on a low box, stand explosively
  • Goblet squat — weight in front forces upright torso
  • Free deep squat, heels down, 2 min hold
Daily habit: Spend 5–10 min/day in a deep squat. Read your phone there. Ankle mobility is the usual limiter — add ankle circles and calf stretching every session.
Tier 2

Deep Cossack Squat

Lateral hip mobility drill and single-leg strength builder. The direct stepping stone to the pistol squat.

Prerequisites

  • Deep Squat

Regression Ladder

  • Wide stance squat shifting side to side — shallow depth
  • Cossack squat with elevated heel on working side
  • Cossack squat holding onto support (pole or TRX)
  • Bodyweight Cossack squat, full depth, 5×5 each side
Key focus: Keep the working foot flat. The extended leg's toes point up. Go as deep as your hip lets you — don't force it, but accumulate volume near your end range.
Tier 2

Archer Push Ups

Unilateral pushing strength — each arm takes most of your bodyweight independently. Gateway to handstand push ups and one-arm push up territory.

Prerequisites

  • Regular Push Ups (20+ clean reps)

Regression Ladder

  • Wide push ups — extra width = more load on each arm
  • Archer push up on knees — reduce bodyweight load
  • Archer push up with fist on a low block (elevated support side)
  • Slow negative archer push up (3–5 s down, push up normally)
  • Full archer push up, 3×8 each side
Wrist care: Use push-up handles or fists if wrists ache. Strengthen the wrist extensors specifically — they're underdeveloped in most gym-goers.
Tier 2

Archer Pull Ups

The direct precursor to the muscle up. One arm bends fully while the other stays extended — teaches you to pull to one side of your body, which is exactly the muscle up motion.

Prerequisites

  • 2 Min Dead Hang
  • 10+ strict pull ups

Regression Ladder

  • Wide-grip pull ups — 15+ clean reps
  • Uneven pull ups (one hand on towel slung over bar)
  • Archer pull up with band assisting the bending arm
  • Slow archer pull up negatives — 5 s down
  • Full archer pull ups, 3×5 each side
Lat vs. bicep: Think "elbow to hip" not "chin to bar." The lat drives the movement. If your bicep gives out first, you haven't found the lat yet.
Tier 3

Pistol Squat

Full single-leg squat to the ground. Requires the full package: ankle mobility, hip flexor flexibility, quad/glute strength, and balance.

Prerequisites

  • Deep Squat
  • Deep Cossack Squat

Regression Ladder

  • Assisted pistol (hold TRX or ring) — full depth with support
  • Box pistol — squat to a box (reduces depth, builds pattern)
  • Elevated heel pistol — plates under heel reduce ankle demand
  • Counterbalance pistol — arms extended forward for balance
  • Full pistol squat, 5×3 each side
Hamstring flexibility: The free leg must stay horizontal. If it drifts down or you round your back, your hamstrings are the limiter — work Jefferson curls and RDL stretches.
Tier 3

Muscle Up

The king of bar work. A pull up that transitions into a dip on top of the bar. Requires pulling power, an explosive "hip-to-bar" kip or strict strength, and shoulder mobility in the turnover.

Prerequisites

  • Archer Pull Ups (5 each side)
  • 15+ strict pull ups
  • Bar dips (10+)

Regression Ladder

  • High pull ups — pull until bar is at chest/navel
  • Jumping muscle up — jump to above bar, lower slowly
  • Banded muscle up — band reduces load on the pull
  • Kipping muscle up — use swing for momentum (acceptable step)
  • Strict muscle up — no swing, pure strength
The transition is the wall: Most people can pull high enough but can't rotate the wrists forward. Practice "false grip" (wrist over bar) on hangs and ring rows to get comfortable in that position.
Tier 3

L-Sit on Parallettes

Isometric hold with legs horizontal and body above the supports. Brutal on hip flexors, triceps, and core compression. Foundation of the entire gymnastics press world.

Prerequisites

  • 2 Min Dead Hang
  • Dip strength (push-down)

Regression Ladder

  • Tuck L-sit — knees pulled to chest, feet off ground — 10 s holds
  • One-leg L-sit — one leg extended, one tucked
  • L-sit with heels dragging floor — partial load off hip flexors
  • Parallette push-down hold (body above bars, legs angled)
  • Full L-sit, 3×10–15 s, building to 30 s
Hip flexor weakness vs. compression: Most people lack hip flexor strength to hold the legs horizontal, not just core. Add hanging leg raises and seated leg lifts to target this specifically.
Tier 3

Bent Knee Dragon Flag

Body supported only at shoulders, with bent knees. The accessible version of Bruce Lee's dragon flag. Teaches the entire posterior chain to work as a rigid plank.

Prerequisites

  • L-Sit (10 s+)
  • Strong hollow body hold

Regression Ladder

  • Hollow body hold on floor — 30 s with no sag
  • Negative dragon flag — tuck, then slowly lower with straight body
  • Bent knee dragon flag hold — 3–5 s isometric in lowered position
  • Bent knee dragon flag, slow negative — 5 s down
  • Straight leg dragon flag — ultimate form
No hip sag allowed: The moment your hips break, the movement is over. It's better to hold a tucked position perfectly than a partially extended one that sags. Quality over range.
Tier 3

Handstand Walk

Freestanding handstand with controlled forward movement. Requires solid static handstand and the ability to shift weight through the fingertips to correct balance.

Prerequisites

  • Freestanding Handstand (30 s)

Regression Ladder

  • Wall handstand — back to wall, build shoulder endurance
  • Chest-to-wall handstand — harder, more upright position
  • Handstand kick-up and balance attempts — off the wall
  • Freestanding handstand hold — 10 s → 20 s → 30 s
  • Handstand walk — small steps, maintain alignment
Balance is in the fingers: You correct a falling handstand by pressing through the fingertips (falling forward) or the heel of the palm (falling backward). Train "finger push-ups" and wrist flexibility heavily.
Tier 4

Handstand Push Ups

Overhead pressing against full bodyweight from a handstand. Tremendous shoulder, tricep, and upper trap strength. Also demands a solid handstand base.

Prerequisites

  • Archer Push Ups (8 each side)
  • Freestanding Handstand (20 s+)

Regression Ladder

  • Pike push ups — feet on floor, hips high, head between arms
  • Elevated pike push ups — feet on box (more weight on arms)
  • Headstand push ups — balance on head as a rest point
  • Wall HSPU negative — kick to wall, lower slowly to head
  • Full wall HSPU — press back to top
  • Freestanding HSPU — no wall, balance demands peak
Head position: Don't go straight down — allow a slight forward lean and touch the floor just in front of your hands. This is the proper tripod base and protects the cervical spine.
Tier 4

Human Flag

Body held horizontal off a vertical pole using lateral pushing and pulling. One of the most visually impressive skills and a true test of full-body lateral strength.

Prerequisites

  • 2 Min Dead Hang
  • Bent Knee Dragon Flag
  • Strong pull ups and dips

Regression Ladder

  • Tuck human flag — both knees pulled to chest, hold 3–5 s
  • One-leg out human flag — one leg extended, one tucked
  • Straddle human flag — legs split wide (reduces lever)
  • Bent-knee human flag hold — knees slightly bent
  • Full human flag — 3–5 s hold, building to 10 s+
Top arm pushes, bottom arm pulls: The top arm is in a full lateral push, the bottom arm is pulling. Most beginners don't push hard enough on top. Think about crushing the pole with both hands while pushing the ceiling away with the top arm.
Tier 4

Backflip

Full backward rotation in the air from standing. Primarily a jump mechanics and commitment issue — not a strength issue. Fear is the main blocker once you have the base fitness.

Prerequisites

  • Deep Squat (explosive base)
  • Tuck jumps (air awareness)
  • Comfortable back bend / bridge

Regression Ladder

  • Back rolls on the floor — get comfortable with backward rotation
  • Trampoline back tuck — same movement, no floor consequence
  • Spotted backflip — coach / friend holds your hip and shoulder
  • Backflip into foam pit (gymnastics gym) — remove fear variable
  • Backflip on crash mat, then grass, then floor
The physics: Jump UP first (not back), then tuck aggressively. Most failed backflips are under-rotated because the person jumped backward instead of vertical. Train box jumps and squat jumps for the explosive component.
Tier 5

Muscle Up into Handstand on Bar

After completing the muscle up, you continue pressing overhead until your body inverts into a handstand on top of the bar. Requires precise strength and balance at full extension above the bar.

Prerequisites

  • Muscle Up (strict, 3+)
  • Freestanding Handstand (30 s)
  • Bar dip to full lockout

Regression Ladder

  • Muscle up + hold support position (above bar) — build pressing confidence
  • From support: lean forward slowly, shift weight to wrists
  • Spotted press to handstand on bar
  • Low bar handstand (bar at waist height) — easier kick-up
  • Full bar handstand kick-up from muscle up
Wrist shift is critical: As you press up, your wrists must rotate from a thumbs-forward bar grip to a thumbs-backward grip supporting a handstand. Practice this rotation in isolation first while standing.
Tier 5

L-Sit Press to Handstand

From an L-sit, compress into a tuck, then slowly press upward into a handstand — no kick, no jump. Pure pressing strength through an extreme range. Arguably the hardest press in gymnastics.

Prerequisites

  • L-Sit (20 s+)
  • Freestanding Handstand (30 s)
  • Handstand Push Ups (5+)
  • Pike flexibility (flat pike sit)

Regression Ladder

  • Tuck planche — lean forward from tuck, shift weight above wrists
  • Tuck press to handstand — press from tucked position (shorter lever)
  • Straddle press to handstand — legs wide reduces lever significantly
  • Negative straddle press — hold handstand, slowly lower to straddle L
  • Full L-sit press (pike press) to handstand
Compression is the limiter: You need to fold your torso onto your thighs with straight legs (compression flexibility). Add daily pancake stretches, pike sits with dorsiflexion, and standing forward folds. This alone takes months.

Recommended focus order: Start simultaneously on Tier 1 (dead hang & deep squat daily). Move to Tier 2 once you have 10 pull ups and can squat deep. Run all tracks in parallel — legs, pull, push, core. Don't wait to "finish" one track before starting another.

Timeline reality check: With consistent training (4–5 sessions/week), expect 6–12 months to reach Tier 3, 18–24 months to Tier 4, and 3+ years to master Tier 5 skills. The implied prerequisites (pull ups, push ups, handstand) should be trained concurrently from day one.
